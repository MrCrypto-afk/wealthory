What Is a CAGR Calculator?

A CAGR calculator is a financial planning tool that computes the Compound Annual Growth Rate of an investment. CAGR represents the rate at which an investment would have grown if it had compounded at a steady rate every year from its initial value to its final value. The formula used is: CAGR = (FV / PV)^(1/n) - 1, where FV is the final value, PV is the initial value (present value), and n is the number of years. This metric is widely used by analysts, fund managers, and individual investors to evaluate and compare investment performance across different time horizons.

Why Is CAGR Important?

CAGR is essential because it eliminates the noise of year-to-year fluctuations and provides a single, smoothed growth rate. This makes it far more meaningful than simple average returns, which can be misleading. For example, if a fund gains 50% in year one and loses 30% in year two, the simple average return is 10%, but the actual compounded growth (CAGR) is only about 2.5%. CAGR gives you the true picture of how your money actually grew over the entire period.

How to Use CAGR for Investment Decisions

  • Compare Funds: Use CAGR to compare two mutual funds over the same period, ensuring an apples-to-apples comparison regardless of volatility differences.
  • Set Realistic Goals: If large-cap equity funds historically deliver 12% CAGR, you can project how much you need to invest today to reach your target corpus.
  • Track Portfolio Performance: Calculate the CAGR of your overall portfolio to see if your asset allocation strategy is meeting your expected growth rate.
  • Benchmark Against Inflation: A positive real return (CAGR minus inflation) ensures your purchasing power is genuinely increasing over time.

Limitations of CAGR

While CAGR is a powerful metric, it has limitations. It assumes a smooth growth trajectory and does not reflect interim volatility or drawdowns. An investment with a high CAGR may have experienced significant drops during the period, which CAGR alone won't reveal. Additionally, CAGR is a backward-looking measure — past growth rates do not guarantee future performance. It's best used alongside other metrics like standard deviation, Sharpe ratio, and maximum drawdown for a comprehensive investment analysis.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can CAGR be negative?

Yes. CAGR will be negative when the final value of an investment is lower than the initial value, indicating that the investment lost value over the measured period. For example, if you invested Rs 10,00,000 and the value dropped to Rs 7,00,000 over 3 years, the CAGR would be approximately -11.2%.

What is the difference between CAGR and IRR?

CAGR measures the growth of a single lumpsum investment from start to finish, while Internal Rate of Return (IRR) accounts for multiple cash flows at different points in time. If your investment involves periodic additions or withdrawals, IRR is the more appropriate metric. CAGR is best suited for evaluating a one-time investment held over a specific period.
