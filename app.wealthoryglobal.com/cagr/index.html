Understanding Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR)

The Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) is one of the most accurate ways to calculate and determine returns for anything that can rise or fall in value over time. Investors use CAGR to compare the performance of various investments over different time horizons.

The CAGR Formula

CAGR = [(End Value / Beginning Value)(1/n) - 1] × 100

Where:
n = Number of years

Why use CAGR?

Investments rarely grow at a steady, flat rate. They experience volatility—going up 20% one year and down 10% the next. CAGR smooths out this volatility and gives you a single, representational number showing what your annual return would be if growth had been completely steady.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good CAGR for stocks?

Historically, broad market index funds (like the S&P 500 or Nifty 50) have generated a CAGR of around 10% to 12% over long periods (15+ years), before inflation.

Does CAGR account for regular investments?

No. Standard CAGR only calculates the growth between a single initial lump-sum investment and its final value. If you make regular deposits (like an SIP), you should use an XIRR (Extended Internal Rate of Return) calculation instead.
