The Hidden Cost of Procrastination

When it comes to wealth creation, time is a far more powerful lever than capital. The "Cost of Delay" is the massive opportunity cost incurred when you postpone investing. Because compound interest acts as an exponential curve, chopping off the final years of your investment horizon destroys the most lucrative segment of your wealth accumulation.

Why does a 1-year delay cost so much over 25 years?

A common misconception is that if you delay investing by one year, you only lose the first year's returns. In reality, you are losing the last year's compounded returns. The magic of compounding happens in the later years. When your money doubles, the absolute value of that doubling is exponentially larger in year 25 than in year 1. Delaying by one year pushes your entire wealth curve backward.

The Mechanics of Compound Interest

To understand the severe impact of delaying, look at the math. If you invest ₹10,000 per month at 12% for 20 years, your corpus will be approximately ₹1 Crore. But if you invest for 25 years, it becomes ₹1.9 Crores. That 5-year difference didn't just add a few lakhs—it nearly doubled the entire portfolio size.

How can I recover from a delayed start?

The only mathematical way to bridge the gap caused by a delayed start is to significantly increase your monthly investment amount (via a Step-Up SIP) or aggressively seek higher-yield assets. However, both approaches require either sacrificing more current income or taking on substantially higher risk.
